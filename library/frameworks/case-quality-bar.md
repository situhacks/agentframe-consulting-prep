# Case quality bar — good vs bad

The "what separates a strong answer from a weak one" reference, for coaching during `case-reviewer` (and for
nudging in `run-case`). Knowing the framework *names* isn't enough — this is the standard a student is graded
against. (Distilled from gcamilo/management-consulting — see README for credit.)

---

## Issue trees / structure

**A weak structure looks like this** — and here's why it loses points:
- **Not MECE.** Buckets overlap ("Risks" alongside "Competition" — competition *is* a risk) or there's a
  catch-all ("Other considerations") that signals incomplete thinking.
- **Generic.** "Is the market big? Is it growing?" applies to any market entry anywhere — nothing specific to
  *this* company, product, or situation.
- **No decision path.** Reading the tree doesn't tell you what analysis would actually resolve the question.

**A strong structure:**
- **MECE and specific.** Branches don't overlap and together cover the whole problem; each is tailored to the
  actual case ("US TAM for our ICP: mid-market logistics, 200–2000 employees", not "is the market big?").
- **Hypothesis-driven.** The tree is built to *test* something, not just list topics. Each leaf points to the
  data or calculation that would resolve it.
- **Evidence-aware.** Distinguishes what's known from what's assumed (see `evidence-standards.md` — [F]/[I]/[A]/[E]).

When reviewing: did they tailor the structure to THIS case, or bolt on a canned framework? Canned = a 2-3 on structure.

## Recommendations / synthesis

**A weak recommendation:** "The company should improve customer experience to reduce churn by investing in
customer success and the product." Why it fails:
- **Not specific** — a platitude, not a recommendation.
- **No owner, no timeline, no evidence, no risks, no "so what."**

**A strong recommendation has these moves** (the standard to coach toward):
- **BOTTOM LINE** — the answer first, in one sentence, with the key number. ("Halt the NRR decline by Q3 via a
  save program for the 23 high-risk accounts = 68% of projected churn revenue.")
- **SO WHAT** — 2–3 reasons, each evidence-coded ([F]/[I]/[A]), with the root cause named, not just symptoms.
- **KEY RISKS** — what could make this wrong, with a rough probability and a mitigation.
- **NEXT ACTIONS** — who does what by when (owner + deadline). Specific.
- **COUNTER-ARGUMENT** — what a smart skeptic would say, and why you still hold (or where they're right). This
  is the move that separates a strong candidate — it shows you stress-tested your own answer.

When reviewing: did they lead with the answer and the "so what", or bury it? Did they name a root cause or list
platitudes? Did they pressure-test their own recommendation, or just assert it?

---

## The one habit that matters most
Across structure and recommendation: **state what's a fact vs. an assumption, and challenge your own answer.**
A candidate who says "I'm assuming 15% growth — if it's under 10%, this flips" beats one who states a guess as
a fact. That's the orchestrate-and-audit posture, and it's what the modern AI-integrated interviews test for.
